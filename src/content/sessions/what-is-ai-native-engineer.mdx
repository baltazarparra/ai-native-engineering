## What defines an AI-Native Engineer

Quick test: imagine your workday without any AI. If nothing really changes, AI is just a sidekick you call once in a while. An AI-Native Engineer works in a way where AI is part of the kitchen, not a fancy gadget on the shelf. Three things define this profile:

1. **Knowing what to hand off and what to do yourself**. AI is great at first drafts, listing options, and grinding through repetitive stuff. It has no clue what your team's priority is, or what's actually worth building this week.

2. **Having a way of working, not just an app installed**. A tool without a process is like buying a fancy oven and still eating cereal for dinner.

3. **Double-checking everything AI gives you**. It produces code that runs and text that sounds smart. But "sounds smart" is not the same as "is right".

This applies to devs, QA, PMs, designers. It's not about coding better. It's about changing how you work.

### What changed

Back in the day, the senior dev on the team stood out because they typed fast, remembered library docs by heart, and recognized patterns they'd seen a hundred times. AI does most of that for you now. What got more valuable: explaining clearly what you need, judging if the result makes sense, and seeing how the pieces of the system fit together. Thinking matters more than typing.

### What didn't change

The basics are still the basics. If you don't get how a database actually works, you won't notice that the AI's shiny query will explode the moment real users show up. If you don't understand what the user needs, you can't explain it to AI either.

Think of AI like a megaphone. If you have something clear to say, it makes you louder. If you're confused, it just broadcasts your confusion further.

## Why this matters

Most people in tech already use AI here and there: autocomplete while typing, a chat to ask quick questions, generating a chunk of text. But poking at a tool now and then is very different from making it part of how you work.

The gap between "I use AI" and "I work AI-native" is like the gap between asking a friend for a recipe once versus actually learning to cook. One gives you dinner tonight. The other changes how you eat for the rest of your life.

## Real example

Here's how an AI-Native Engineer tackles a task:

1. Before opening the AI, writes down what needs to be done, how it should behave, and what must not happen
2. Hands that description to an AI tool that already knows the project
3. Reads the result carefully — doesn't accept everything on the first try
4. Asks the AI to write tests based on the original description
5. When something isn't right, gives specific feedback instead of just hitting "try again"

The difference isn't the tool. It's the process.

## Where this breaks

Watch out for these traps:

- **Accepting everything without reading**: the AI produces stuff that looks good, but "looks good" doesn't mean "is right". If you accept without checking, you didn't delegate the work, you delegated the blame.
- **Asking before thinking**: "do this for me" isn't a useful instruction. The vaguer your ask, the more generic and useless the answer.
- **Thinking AI replaces experience**: AI speeds up people who know what they want. For people who don't, it just speeds up the confusion.

> A great tool with a bad process gives you bad results, faster.

## Takeaway

- Before opening the AI, write down what you want, the limits, and how you'll know the result is good.
- Find where AI actually helps your work — not where everyone uses it because it's trendy.
- Check everything AI produces like it came from someone still learning the ropes. If you can't tell good from bad, study the topic first. The tool comes later.
